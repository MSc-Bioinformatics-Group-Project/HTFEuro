Statistical Tests

Nucleotide Diversity

Nucleotide diversity is a concept in molecular genetics measuring the degree of polymorphism within a population. This is simply defined as the average number of nucleotide differences per site between two DNA sequences in all possible pairs within the same population, (this is denoted as Pi). Within populations, highly diverse or well-balanced libraries have approximately equal populations of all four nucleotides. Conversely, low diversity libraries have a high proportion of certain nucleotides. Within NPEuro portal we are able to provide nucleotide diversity between SNPs comparing different populations, denoted as Pi within the results table.

Haplotype Diversity

SNPEuro also provides a calculation for haplotype diversity. A haplotype is a set of DNA polymorphisms that tend to be inherited together and can be a combination of alleles or set of SNPs found on the same chromosome. Haplotype diversity represents the probability that two randomly sampled alleles are different and can be implicated by a number of different processes such as mutation, recombination, marker ascertainment and demography. It is thus the measure of the uniqueness of a particular haplotype in a given population. Within SNPEuro you can select for this statistic to be read from their chosen genomic and/or population searches.

Genetic Differentiation ( FST ) & Divergence (d_XY)

You can select different European populations to deliver calculated population genetic variation. This measure, also called the fixation index ( FST ), is denoted as the proportion of the total genetic variance contained in a subpopulation relative to the total genetic variance. Population selection strongly influences ( FST ) values, such that closely related groups are indistinguishable, displaying panmixis.

Within genetic diversity, (d_XY), is the absolute nucleotide divergence between two populations, which is also calculated within SNPEuro. This measure shows the patterns of genetic divergence between populations as a function of nucleotide diversity.

Tajima’s D

Neutrality tests compare two estimators of the population mutation parameter that characterises the mutation-drift equilibrium. The Tajima’s D test is one measure of neutrality and is computed as the difference between two measures of genetic diversity. It is the mean number of pairwise differences and the number of segregating sites, each scaled such that they are expected to be the same in neutrally evolving population of constant size. Tajima’s D thus distinguishes between a DNA sequence evolving randomly (or neutrally) and one evolving under a non-random process. A negative result signifies an excess of low frequency polymorphisms relative to expectation (population size expansion), whereas a positive result signifies low levels of both low and high frequency polymorphisms, indicating a contraction in population size and/or balancing selection.
